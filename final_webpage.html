Land Use In
South Africa

Overview History Agriculture Socio Economic Biodiversity and Conservation Mining Forestry

Overview

South Africa’s land use is shaped by its diverse geography and complex economic and social history. Spanning nearly 1.2 million square kilometers, South Africa is rich in both biodiversity and cultural diversity, boasting a variety of landscapes and inhabitants1. The country is divided into nine provinces (fig 1), although nearly half of the population reside in just two: Gauteng and KwaZulu Natal (fig 2). As a result of its political and economic history, South Africa is the most urbanized country in sub-Saharan Africa, with approximately 62% of the population living in urban areas1.

South Africa’s varied ecosystems, including plateaus, plains, bush, and deserts, support a host of plant and animal species (fig 3), much of which are endemic to the region4. The country is well known for its abundant wildlife, protected through well-established parks and reserves that support many opportunities for wildlife tourism.

While land cover is mainly grassland, cropland takes up a significant portion of land use due to the country’s moderate climate, which is suitable to agriculture4. Despite its sustainability goals, South Africa faces significant land issues; almost 60% of the land is degraded, and 91% is prone to desertification5. Major threats to biodiversity stem from agriculture, human development and settlements, plantation forestry, mining, and infrastructure development4. These issues are rooted in the country’s long history of colonialism and systemic racism that shaped land use and ownership patterns1. The legacy of apartheid left pervasive inequalities in land ownership and access that influence how land is managed and used today1. Land use is highly influenced by development goals and natural resource management, but in a post-Apartheid society, the land management system and spatial landscape is highly fragmented and disjointed6. Balancing natural resource management, historic land ownership patterns, and competing demands of development, agriculture, and conservation remains a complex and ongoing challenge for South Africa.

History

South African land has a highly divisive history shaped by over a century of colonialism and apartheid. The Group Areas Act of 1950 created “homelands” for the sole occupa-tion of different racial and ethnic groups with buffer zones separating areas inhabited by white South Africans (fig 4)5. The act de-integrated rural and urban areas by mandating forced removal and relocation of nearly 3.5 million people5. White urban cores allowed only minor black populations, creating rural, peri-urban, and metropolitan racially segregated areas1. Informal settlements often appeared in buffer zones as black, Indian, and mixed townships were established outside of city centers (fig 5)1.

Black settlements and rural areas had little to no management during Apartheid1. The resulting environmental consequences were devastating to land and soil quality6. Subsistence in homelands relied on small agricultural plots; with high population and livestock densities living within these confined spaces, rangelands became severely degraded, causing erosion issues that the nation faces today5. Resource based livelihoods are disproportionately vulnerable to land degradation, furthering contributing to climate related poverty5.

Apartheid systems created land use patterns that are difficult to fully dismantle and resolve even three decades later. At its close, white South Africans held 84% of non-public land, and formally white urban areas retained political and economic control1. Apartheid’s legacy has left its mark on agriculture as well. Large commercial farms are generally managed by white South Africans, and smaller, subsistence farms in communal areas are predominantly owned by Black farmers1. Land reform has been slow; only 3 million of 82 million hectares have been reclaimed, perpetuating historic land management inequality and unequal access to natural resources and continuing environmental racism1. Post apartheid reforms have struggled to reconcile national and international commitments to address degradation, reduce carbon emissions, and reach Sustainable Development Goals5.

Climate change, historic land tenure, and limited post-apartheid reforms have created a perfect storm for land degradation. In addition to erosion, communal rangelands are under threat from woody encroachment5. In response to increased tree and shrub densities in savanna ecosystems, rangeland productivity declined5. Reduced water supply, carrying capacity, biodiversity, and carbon storage capacity disrupt traditional grassland ecosystem functions5.

Agriculture

Agriculture plays a significant role in South Africa’s economy, occupying approximately 10% of its land area4. However, South Africa’s agricultural potential is relatively low compared to other regions of the globe. Due to the semi-arid climate, only 12% of land is considered arable despite 81.7% being classified as agricultural9. Agricultural activities primarily include livestock farming- particularly cattle and goats- maize, wheat, citrus, sugarcane and wine grape production5. Crop distribution varies across the country, with the Western Cape contributing fruit and viticulture, while the Highveld produces maize5. Agricultural distribution is characterized by co-ops in estates, and subsistence farms in rural areas10.

Over the past 20 years, cropland decreased by about 22%, indicating a potential shift towards a post-agrarian society10. Consumption patterns in South Africa reflect a mix of traditional and contemporary preferences, although the traditional diet is facing an increasing demand for processed foods1. During the same period, agricultural production has increasingly shifted towards soybean, with soybean cropland increasing by 679% while maize and wheat cropland declined by 43% and 42% respectively10.

South Africa’s agricultural sector is the most developed in Africa, positioning the nation as a leading exporter within the region11,1. However, the sector faces significant challenges driven by biophysical, institutional, and socioeconomic factors, including water scarcity, inconsistent energy supply, land degradation, and the impacts of climate change1,5. Climate change, in particular, has increased the likelihood of severe droughts in arable lands as average temperatures soar and rainfall declines (fig 6)11. These issues are exacerbated by historic land management inequality and unequal access to natural resources5.

High value crops are grown in areas with more secure water resources due to their need for intensive irrigation. Rainfed agriculture can be found in the regions with the highest rainfall, such as Kwa-Zulu Natal, Mpumalanga, and the Eastern Cape10. Dry regions concentrate on livestock production and wildlife farming, as agricultural production is limited by scare water resources which limit irrigation capacity10. Balancing land and water requirements is a complex issue when considering the impacts of agriculture. Sugarcane, for instance, is a land and water intensive monocrop that relies heavily on fertilizers and pesticides13. Continuous heavy reliance on nitrogenous fertilizers lowers soil pH, eventually leading to acidification and buildup of heavy metals13. This soil degradation and fertility loss threaten an already precarious percentage of arable land in South Africa13. Additionally, the production of sugarcane releases significant carbon when fields are burned during harvesting13.

Socio Economic

Recent development and urbanization trends in South Africa have significantly reshaped land use patterns, driven by factors such as economic opportunity, population growth, and historic migration trends, particularly in the years post-Apartheid. The country’s urban population is on the rise (fig 7), particularly in major cities like Johannesburg, Durban, and Cape Town14. Urban sprawl puts pressure on land resources and presents complex challenges for urban planning and development, especially with the continued growth of informal settlements15.

South Africa’s status as one of the most industrialized nations on the continent can be attributed to its strong tourism industry, with popular destinations like Cape Town and Kruger National Park attracting international visitors1,16. The services sector dominates the nation’s GDP, which has grown 3.3% in the past two decades16. Despite economic growth, unemployment remains high at over 25%17. Regarded as one of the most unequal countries globally, the wealth gap is remarkable: the top 0.01% hold 86% of total net wealth, while the bottom 50% of the population holds negative net worth17. Urban population growth has surged in response to GDP, rising by approximately 5% over the last decade from 63.79% to 68.82% in 202318. Notably, due to wealth distribution and historic land trends, 26% of urban dwellers live in informal settlements, with this number continually growing19. Although many of these settlements originated from forced segregation during the Apartheid era, rapid urbanization has further deepened socio-economic inequalities and economic barriers remain prevalent, leading to further growth in informal settlements1.

Informal settlements are frequently located near riverbanks, flood plains, and wetlands, placing them at disproportionate risk for environmental hazards, particularly floods19. In Cape Town, for instance, informal settlements are concentrated in the Cape Flats, low-lying areas to the south and east19. Khayelitsha, located outside Cape Town, is the largest informal settlement in the country, with 391,747 people living on 38.71 km²20. These densely populated settlements are often built illegally on government land and virtually nonexistent land regulation results in encroachment into natural environments15. Significant pressure has been put on the fynbos biome in the Western Cape, an environmentally critical shrubland ecosystem that is recognized globally as a biodiversity hotspot21. Not only does the unregulated expansion of informal settlements fragment and destroy the fragile fynbos ecosystem, it threatens the biodiversity and soil structure of the impacted land as well21.

Biodiversity and Conservation

South Africa is considered one of the world’s most biologically diverse nations, home to 10% of the world’s plant species and 7% of reptile, bird, and mammal species22. Endemism rates are exceptionally high, with 56% of amphibians, 65% of plants, and almost 70% of invertebrates found only in South Africa22. Given its rich biodiversity, protected areas (PA’s) play a crucial role in conservation, yet fencing may have unintended consequences23.

When implemented and managed well, fences around protected areas can benefit both wildlife and nearby human communities24. Protected areas in South Africa are often situated near local communities and agricultural activities, where demarcation can reduce encroachment and protect crops and livestock from grazing and predators23. In reality, local communities are rarely involved in the decision-making process when creating a PA, causing friction25. Fences disrupt traditional ways of life by limiting access to resources, altering travel routes, and, in some cases, displacing communities25. For many, fences are reminders of colonial and Apartheid era policies of land appropriation and exclusion, underscoring the importance of including affected communities in the planning and creation of PA’s23.

From an ecological perspective, fences inhibit migration and dispersal, increasing pressure on species distribution and contributing to die offs of ungulates and other mammals when resources become scare23. These trends are expected to intensify with climate change, as animals may need to travel further to find adequate food and water23. Further, protected areas must manage populations to prevent inbreeding and ensure genetic diversity, particularly in smaller areas23.

The security of fenced protected areas is also a concern as impermeability depends on species-specific behavioral responses and the condition and construction of fences which require substantial funding to maintain23. While rhinos and buffalo are easily contained, for instance, leopards and digging species like warthogs require more sophisticated containment infrastructure23. For animals such as elephants and lions that rely on vast territories to maintain healthy populations, fragmentation is especially harmful, sometimes leading to destructive feeding behaviors and other stress responses23. However, fragmentation is not just a result of fencing; as human population grows, the developed landscape grows with it, encroaching on wildlife habitats. Protected areas remain crucial in conservation efforts, and as private game reserves cover 16% of the country (fig 8), it is important that connectivity is ensured between these areas24.

Mining

Mining in South Africa plays a crucial role in the country’s economic growth, contributing to job creation and GDP27. The country is home to 522 mines and over 22 minerals; specifically, South Africa is the largest producer of coal, platinum, gold, and diamonds28.

The nation’s mineral wealth has driven mining for over a century with little to no regard for the environment, thus the socio-environmental consequences are substantial29. The long history of the industry has shaped legislature surrounding mining activities; regulatory gaps, poor enforcement, and biased land-use decisions continue to jeopardize both communities and the environment27. This poses significant environmental and social challenges, particularly for communities in mining-affected regions29.

Although there are regulations concerning mining activities and their impacts, mining companies in South Africa frequently neglect environmental laws, resulting in severe degradation of local ecosystems29. The Human Rights Commission notes that mining often commences prematurely, depriving communities of bargaining power and impacting their rights to fair compensation and relocation30. Further, the lack of stringent land-use guidelines allows mining companies to repurpose post-closure land arbitrarily30. Mining companies opt for the easiest and most cost-effective land restoration when mines close, rather than restore them to their post mining condition30. Degraded areas are often designated for low-cost housing or grazing which rarely meets the original restoration goals30.

Unfortunately, competing land-use policies tend to support mining activities over other sectors, such as agriculture and conservation, in favour of development and economic investments30. In the Mpumalanga province, the Department of Mineral Resources (DMR) accepts applications for rights (fig 9), despite the importance of agriculture in the region29. Due to the lack of arable soil in the country, this raises particularly poignant concerns for soil health and food security29.

Biodiversity is another casualty of mining expansion (Maze). In biodiversity-rich areas, mining activities compete directly with conservation efforts32. The DMR approves mining permits without local government consultation, enabling land transformation without stringent land-use controls32. This contributes to an irreversible loss of habitat in regions with high conservation value29. The long-term environmental impact has been profound. Strip mining leaves permanent scars on the landscape, leading to soil erosion, destruction of vegetation, and widespread land degradation29. In eMmalahleni, Mpumalanga, coal mining has degraded approximately 3.2 million hectares of land, with a slow restoration rate of only 10-12%29.

Forestry

South Africa's forests cover approximately 32.7% of the country’s land surface, comprising three main categories: indigenous (natural) forests, wooded savannahs (woodlands), and industry (plantation) forests33. The country’s forests are protected by relatively strong legislation with the National Forests Act (1998) and the National Veld and Forest Fire Act (1998)33. These laws aim to protect forests by promoting conservation, sustainable development, and equitable distribution of their benefits33. These efforts recognize the economic, social, and environmental importance of forests while addressing the need to balance conservation with resource use33. Despite their importance, South Africa's forests face significant environmental and economic challenges. Tree cover declined significantly in the past two decades (fig 10, fig 11), with KwaZulu-Natal and Mpumalanga contributing to 70% of the total tree loss34.

South Africa is predominantly an unforested country, with indigenous forests constituting its smallest biome35. Natural forests account for only 0.9% of the total land area, and are small, fragmented, and dependent on a climate with no frost and high seasonal rainfall33. These forests play a critical role in biodiversity conservation and soil protection33.

Woodlands, the largest category by area, span 39 million hectares and are primarily found on communal lands, where they provide resources such as wood, fuel, and food to local communities33. However, they are often poorly managed and heavily exploited due to their extent and vastness33.

Plantation forests, covering 1.19 million hectares, are concentrated in provinces like KwaZulu-Natal, Mpumalanga, and the Eastern and Western Cape34. Forestry mainly employs exotic species like pine, eucalyptus, and wattle, to meet timber needs35. These exotic trees are considered invasive due to extensive geographic reach, population size, and negative impact of indigenous vegetation structures36. Exotic plantations decrease streamflow by about 3% and increase evapotranspiration, altering catchment water yields34. These effects are especially significant in high-rainfall areas that are critical for water catchments, posing a major concern for the nation’s already scare water resources35. These issues prompted in a large-scale government initiative to eradicate invasive species36. The National Water Act (1998) was also amended to regulate land uses, such as forestry, by introducing a category of water use known as 'stream flow reduction activity'35.

Fires pose another critical risk, particularly in plantation forests established on fire-prone grasslands33. In the Western Cape, 17,000 fires were recorded in 2017, 2,000 of which originated in informal settlements34. Although forestry occupies a relatively small area, it contributes significantly to the economy33. Uncontrolled fires impact both local economies and industry, as well as increasing forest vulnerability to pests and diseases33.
